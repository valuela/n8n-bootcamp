# Part I — Program Identity

## Program Title

The Complete n8n Bootcamp

**Subtitle**

From Developer to AI Automation Engineer

---

## Program Purpose

This bootcamp exists to prepare students for professional careers in workflow automation, systems integration, AI-powered business automation, and automation engineering.

Rather than teaching software features, the curriculum develops the ability to solve business problems through well-designed automation systems.

Every lesson, lab, and project contributes toward real-world employability.

---

## Graduate Outcome

Graduates should be capable of independently:

- Understanding business requirements.
- Designing automation architectures.
- Building production-quality n8n workflows.
- Integrating APIs, databases, and AI services.
- Debugging production failures.
- Deploying maintainable automation systems.
- Explaining technical decisions professionally.
- Building a portfolio that demonstrates engineering competency.

---

# Part II — Educational Philosophy

The curriculum follows six educational principles.

## 1. Problem-First Learning

Students encounter the business problem before learning the technology.

Technology should always answer a business need.

---

## 2. Progressive Complexity

Every lesson increases difficulty in a controlled manner.

Students should never encounter concepts that rely on knowledge not yet taught.

---

## 3. Engineering Mindset

Students are trained to think like engineers rather than software operators.

Emphasis is placed on:

- Design decisions
- Trade-offs
- Scalability
- Reliability
- Maintainability

---

## 4. Project-Based Learning

Every module contains practical work.

Knowledge without application is considered incomplete.

---

## 5. Reflection

Every project concludes with a written reflection covering:

- What worked well
- What challenges occurred
- What could be improved
- Alternative solutions

Reflection strengthens engineering judgment.

---

## 6. Professional Readiness

Students are continually prepared for employment through documentation, communication, GitHub practices, architecture discussions, and interview preparation.

---

# Part V — Bloom's Taxonomy

The curriculum follows Bloom's hierarchy.

Students progress through:

1. Remember
2. Understand
3. Apply
4. Analyze
5. Evaluate
6. Create

Every module should move students upward through these levels.

The final capstone sits at the "Create" level.

---

# Part VI — Lesson Blueprint

Every lesson follows this structure.

## 1. Learning Objectives

Written using measurable action verbs.

Examples:

- Design
- Build
- Implement
- Evaluate
- Optimize

Avoid vague verbs such as:

- Learn
- Know
- Understand

---

## 2. Business Context

Why does this automation exist?

Who benefits?

What problem does it solve?

---

## 3. Concept Explanation

Explain the underlying theory before implementation.

---

## 4. Guided Demonstration

Instructor-led implementation.

---

## 5. Guided Lab

Students reproduce the workflow.

---

## 6. Independent Lab

Students extend or modify the solution independently.

---

## 7. Design Challenge

Students receive only business requirements.

No implementation instructions are provided.

---

## 8. Common Mistakes

Real mistakes made by automation engineers.

---

## 9. Debugging Walkthrough

Students diagnose and fix intentionally broken workflows.

---

## 10. Production Considerations

Security

Performance

Scalability

Monitoring

Maintainability

---

## 11. Interview Questions

Technical

Behavioral

Architecture

Scenario-based

---

## 12. Portfolio Enhancement

Students improve documentation, architecture, or workflow quality before committing their work.

# Part VII — Assessment Framework

Student performance is evaluated using five dimensions.

| Dimension              | Weight |
| ---------------------- | ------ |
| Technical Correctness  | 30%    |
| Workflow Design        | 20%    |
| Documentation          | 15%    |
| Debugging Ability      | 15%    |
| Professional Practices | 20%    |

Students are expected to achieve competency rather than simply complete tasks.

---

# Part VIII — Definition of Done

A lesson is complete only when:

- The workflow functions correctly.
- Errors have been tested.
- Documentation is updated.
- Reflection is written.
- Git commit is completed.
- Portfolio artifacts are saved.
- Interview questions can be answered without notes.

Completion means demonstrating professional readiness, not just finishing the exercise.

# Part X — Industry Standards

Students are expected to follow professional engineering practices.

These include:

- Meaningful Git commits
- Semantic versioning
- Consistent naming conventions
- Modular workflow design
- Principle of least privilege for credentials
- Secure secret management
- Code reviews (where applicable)
- Clear documentation

---

# Part XI — Employer Alignment

Each module is mapped to real workplace responsibilities.

- Integration Engineer

- Technical Solutions Engineer

# Part XII — Continuous Improvement

This curriculum is a living document.

New technologies, APIs, AI capabilities, and industry practices should be incorporated through versioned updates while preserving educational consistency.

Major revisions require updates to:

- Learning outcomes
- Competency mappings
- Portfolio standards
- Assessment criteria
- Capstone expectations

Version history must be maintained in CHANGELOG.md.
